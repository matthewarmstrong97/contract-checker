Common traps we flag
💰

"Pay When Paid" Clauses

You don't get paid until they do. If the client goes bust, you're last in line.

🔒

Retention Traps

Your money locked for months with no clear release date or conditions.

📐

Vague Scope

If scope isn't specific, you end up doing extra work for free.

⚡

Liability Traps

You pay for things outside your control — damage caused by others on site.

📅

Delayed Payment Terms

60 or 90-day payment terms hidden in the fine print. Serious cashflow problems.

🚨 Critical Issues
1. Unlimited Liability Clause (Clause 14.3) You could be personally liable with no cap — even for damage caused by other trades.
"Could we cap liability at the value of my subcontract? I'm happy to accept responsibility for my own work, but an open-ended clause may put me at risk for issues outside my control."
2. Pay When Paid (Clause 8.1) This may mean you don't get paid until the head contractor does, which could delay your payment indefinitely.
"Could we agree on a fixed payment date of 20 working days from invoice regardless of upstream payment? This may conflict with CCA 2002."

YOU MAY WANT TO AVOID SIGNING THIS AS IT STANDS

This contract contains clauses that could delay your payment indefinitely and expose you to unlimited liability.

🚨 CRITICAL ISSUES
Issue 1
Pay When Paid Clause
This may mean you don't get paid until the main contractor does, which could delay your payment indefinitely. If the main contractor has cashflow problems or the client disputes payment, you could be left waiting months with no clear recourse.
💬 Suggested question to raise:
"Could we agree on a fixed payment date of 20 working days from invoice regardless of upstream payment?"
Issue 2
Unlimited Liability Clause
You could be held responsible for defects outside your control for up to 10 years. This may expose you to claims well beyond the value of your work, including issues caused by other trades or design problems outside your scope.
💬 Suggested question to raise:
"Could we cap liability at the contract value and limit it to defects caused by my workmanship?"
📌 WHAT TO DO NOW
  1. You may want to avoid signing this contract as it stands.
  2. Raise these concerns with the contractor using the suggested questions above.
  3. If they're unwilling to negotiate, consider walking away or speaking with a construction lawyer.
